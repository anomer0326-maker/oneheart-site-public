Day 31 — Book 1, Chapter 6, §4

0 — Orientation (1 minute) –

Yesterday's homework was an order: come with Psalm 19 already read. Here is why. Yesterday (§3) Calvin argued — from anthropology — that God had to file a written record because our minds leak. Powerful. But an argument. Today he does something a preacher lives for: he shows you that the argument was David's first. §4 is the proof by Scripture of everything §§1–3 claimed. The spectacles were not Calvin's invention; the poet who wrote "the heavens declare the glory of God" is the very poet who turns, in the same breath, to "the law of the Lord is perfect."

So the whole chapter's logic is now standing in front of you, quoted: general revelation (the heavens) → special revelation (the law). Same author, Psalm 19, same page. Calvin's move is to say, look — David makes my transition for me.

Watch the rhetorical machine today. Calvin lines up four witnesses — Psalm 19, Psalm 29, Psalm 93, and the lips of Christ himself (John 4) — and then delivers a one-sentence verdict that swallows the human race. It is a legal brief.

Today's 3 Big Points — I'm telling you now, you'll thank me in Period 4:

  1. after V-ing ..., ‹main-verb› — the long suspended participial preamble. S1 buries three that-clauses inside an after-phrase before the main verb (proceeds) ever arrives. If you can't hold the subject in the air across twenty words, S1 collapses. This is Beveridge's signature suspension.
  2. The trailing nominative absolute as a verdict — the invitation ... proving of no avail (S2), unbelievers being deaf (S3). You met the absolute in Day 13, 18, 22, 30. Today Calvin uses it three times as a closing gavel — a whole clause hung on the end to pronounce sentence.
  3. if not aided ... it necessarily followed that ... — the counterfactual-plus-consequence spine of the verdict (S6). If not = unless; and it necessarily followed that is the logician's ergo. The last sentence is built like a syllogism. Learn its bones.

Books open. Six sentences, four witnesses, one verdict. Let's go.

1 — Full Text (the whole § — 6 sentences, verbatim) –

4. Accordingly, the same prophet, after mentioning that the heavens declare the glory of God, that the firmament sheweth forth the works of his hands, that the regular succession of day and night proclaim his Majesty, proceeds to make mention of the Word:—“The law of the Lord,” says he, “is perfect, converting the soul; the testimony of the Lord is sure, making wise the simple. The statutes of the Lord are right, rejoicing the heart; the commandment of the Lord is pure, enlightening the eyes,” (Psalm 19:1-9). For though the law has other uses besides (as to which, see Book 2 c. 7, sec. 6, 10, 12), the general meaning is, that it is the proper school for training the children of God; the invitation given to all nations, to behold him in the heavens and earth, proving of no avail. The same view is taken in the 29th Psalm, where the Psalmist, after discoursing on the dreadful voice of God, which, in thunder, wind, rain, whirlwind, and tempest, shakes the earth, makes the mountains tremble, and breaks the cedars, concludes by saying, “that in his temple does every one speak of his glory,” unbelievers being deaf to all God’s words when they echo in the air. In like manner another Psalm, after describing the raging billows of the sea, thus concludes, “Thy testimonies are very sure; holiness becometh thine house for ever,” (Psalm 93:5). To the same effect are the words of our Saviour to the Samaritan woman, when he told her that her nation and all other nations worshipped they knew not what; and that the Jews alone gave worship to the true God (John 4:22). Since the human mind, through its weakness, was altogether unable to come to God if not aided and upheld by his sacred word, it necessarily followed that all mankind, the Jews excepted, inasmuch as they sought God without the Word, were labouring under vanity and error.

Examiner's Eye — the trap they will set:

S1 is the hinge, and the trap is to miss the pivot word proceeds. The sentence spends its whole first half on the heavens, firmament, day and night — general revelation. Then one verb turns the corner: proceeds to make mention of the Word. A careless reader treats the Psalm-19 quotation as more of the same (nature-praise). It is the opposite: it is David abandoning the heavens for the law. The distractor will say "Calvin cites Psalm 19 to show the heavens reveal God." No — he cites it to show that even David turned from the heavens to the Word. The point is the pivot, not the panorama.

Second trap, S2: though the law has other uses besides ... the general meaning is. The distractor will make Calvin say the law's only use is instruction. He explicitly says the opposite (other uses besides, cross-referencing Book 2) and then narrows to the general meaning for this argument. Scope, not exclusion.

Third trap, S6: all mankind, the Jews excepted. The claim is not that the Jews were cleverer or more devout. It is that they alone had the Word. The exception is bibliological, not moral. Any choice implying the Jews reached God by superior wisdom is a direction-flip — Calvin's whole point is that no human mind reaches God unaided; the Jews differ only in that they were aided.

4 — Today's Grammar Formulas (this is what gets tested) – 

FORMULA 1 — The suspended 'after V-ing' preamble (find the REAL main verb)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   SUBJECT, after V-ing [that-cl, that-cl, that-cl], + MAIN VERB ...
        = SUBJECT, having first done X, then does Y (the main verb)

   • The finite-looking verbs inside the that-clauses are NOT the main verb.
       "the prophet, after mentioning that the heavens DECLARE ...,
        that the firmament SHEWETH ..., PROCEEDS to make mention of the Word"
       → main verb = PROCEEDS; declare/sheweth are trapped in the after-clause.
   ⚠️ TRAP: accepting the first inflected verb as the predicate. Keep the
      subject in the air until you reach the verb that governs IT.

   Drill (Calvin material):
   → "David, after rehearsing how the heavens blaze, how the seasons turn,
      how the storm breaks the cedars, TURNS at last to the Word."

FORMULA 2 — The trailing nominative absolute as a VERDICT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   [MAIN CLAUSE] ; NOUN + present-participle (+ complement).
        = "— seeing that NOUN is/does ..."  (a causal aside pronouncing sentence)

   • No grammatical tie to the main clause. Read it as "since / seeing that."
   • Calvin fires THREE in a row today:
       the invitation ... PROVING of no avail            (S2)
       unbelievers BEING deaf to all God's words         (S3)
       the Jews EXCEPTED [= the Jews being excepted]      (S6)
   ⚠️ TRAP: trying to attach the participle to the main subject. Don't.
      It floats — and it usually delivers the punch line.

   Drill:
   → "The Word alone forms God's children, the invitation of the heavens
      proving of no avail; the nations being left, apart from Scripture,
      to worship they know not what."

FORMULA 3 — The verdict spine: 'if not' (= unless) + 'it necessarily followed that'
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Since S was unable to V  IF NOT  aided ... ,
        it necessarily followed THAT  [conclusion] .
        = Since S could not V UNLESS aided, therefore [conclusion].

   • 'if not aided' = 'unless aided' — a conditional of NECESSITY (indispensable).
   • 'it necessarily followed that' = logical ergo (entailment, not probability).
   • 'labour under X' = to be BURDENED by X (NOT to work at X).
   ⚠️ TRAP: reading 'if not' as 'except' at sentence level, or 'labouring' as effort.

   Drill:
   → "Since the mind cannot rise to God if not upheld by his word, it
      necessarily followed that the nations, seeking him without Scripture,
      laboured under vanity and error."

vanity (S6) ⚠️ emptiness, futility vanus = empty. Hebrew hebel, Rom. 1:21 ἐματαιώθησαν. NOT conceit.
labour under (S6) ⚠️ to be burdened by, suffer from labor = a load carried. NOT "to work at."
upheld (S6) held up, supported up + hold. Paired with aided = helped forward and kept from falling.
if not (aided) (S6) ⚠️ unless (aided) A conditional of necessity — the Word is indispensable, not merely useful.

6 — Background in 5 Minutes –

The argument of §4 in one line: what Calvin argued in §§1–3, David and Christ already argued for him.

Yesterday Calvin reasoned his way to the necessity of Scripture, from anthropology: we forget, we err, we invent, so God filed a record. That is a syllogism, and a hostile reader can fight a syllogism. So today Calvin does the thing that ends the fight: he shows that the Bible makes his argument itself. He calls four witnesses — Psalm 19, Psalm 29, Psalm 93, and Jesus at the well — and every one of them performs the identical move: begins with the roaring glory of creation, then turns to the Word. Heavens → law. Storm → temple. Sea → testimonies. Nations → the Jews alone. If Scripture's own poets and Scripture's own Lord make the pivot from general to special revelation, then Calvin's whole chapter is not an innovation; it is an exposition.

Psalm 19 is the hinge of the entire chapter — and this is the moment Calvin has been driving toward since §1. Psalm 19 falls famously into two halves: verses 1–6, the heavens declare the glory of God (general revelation); verses 7–14, the law of the Lord is perfect (special revelation). Older critics even thought two separate poems had been stitched together. Calvin's genius is to insist the seam is the point: David himself moves from the sun's circuit to the soul's conversion, from what the eye sees to what the Word does. That single Psalm is Chapters 5 and 6 in miniature — which is exactly why Calvin ended §3 on a Psalm and opened §4 by quoting this one outright. He has walked you from his argument into David's, and they turn out to be the same argument.

Notice the verbs of effect. The heavens declare, shew, proclaim — verbs of announcement. The law converts, makes wise, rejoices, enlightens — verbs of transformation. This is precisely the difference Calvin needs. General revelation tells; special revelation changes. The sky can leave you inexcusable (Rom. 1:20, the verdict of Chapter 5); only the Word can leave you converted. Which is why the open-air invitation ... proves of no avail (S2): an announcement to the deaf accomplishes nothing. The school of the Word is necessary because the broadcast of the heavens, though real and universal, lands on dead ears.

Why John 4:22 is the keystone, not just a fourth prooftext. Three psalmists could be dismissed as Old-Covenant particularism. So Calvin puts the same words in the mouth of Christ: "ye worship ye know not what... the Jews alone." Now the claim is not merely Davidic but dominical, and it is not merely about ancient Israel — it is Jesus, standing in Samaria (the very borderland of nearest approach yet still astray, cf. Day 25), ratifying the thesis of Chapters 3–6: worship without the Word is worship of an unknown god. The Samaritans had five-sixths of the truth and a shortened canon, and it was not enough. The variable, again, is Scripture.

The verdict, and its precision — hold the Barth–Brunner line one last time. S6 pronounces on all mankind: without the Word, the whole race labours under vanity and error. This is as sweeping as Calvin ever gets — and it is not a denial of general revelation. Read the premise: the mind is altogether unable to come to God **if not aided**. The problem is never that the heavens fail to reveal (they blaze — Chapter 5 insisted on it for fifteen sections); the problem is that the mind, through its weakness, cannot make the ascent. Objective revelation, subjective incapacity. The one exception, the Jews, is carved out on purely bibliological grounds: they alone had his sacred word. Delete the Word and they join the verdict. So §4 is not natural theology and not its abolition — it is the same double truth Calvin has held all along: a real revelation, a ruined receiver, and a Word given to bridge the gap.

A note on the law and the three uses. Watch Calvin's honesty in S2: though the law has other uses besides (as to which, see Book 2 c. 7, sec. 6, 10, 12). He will not let Psalm 19 flatten the law into mere instruction. The law also convicts (the usus elenchticus, driving sinners to Christ) and restrains (the civil use); the cross-reference is to his own mature treatment of the three uses of the law — a doctrine Calvin develops more fully and warmly than Luther, especially the tertius usus, the law as the believer's guide. Here he brackets all that: for the present argument, the law's role is to be the school where God's children are trained to know him. Concede the fuller doctrine, then narrow to the point at hand. That discipline — grant the objection, mark the address where you'll pay it, then press on — is worth imitating.

Where we are structurally: §1 spectacles → §2 the archive's history → §3 why the archive was necessary (anthropology) → §4 the archive's necessity proved from Scripture itself (four witnesses + the verdict on the race). Chapter 6 is now essentially complete: without the Word, no one comes to God. Tomorrow Calvin turns the corner into Chapter 7 — the question that §4 forces: if everything hangs on Scripture, then how do we know Scripture is from God? The answer will be the testimony of the Spirit (testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum), the doctrine he has been seeding since Day 26. Come ready.

7 — Scripture Connections –

  1. Psalm 19:1–9 — quoted outright in S1, the chapter's hinge text. Note the two-panel structure: vv.1–6 the heavens declare the glory of God (general revelation) → vv.7–9 the law of the Lord is perfect (special revelation). Calvin's entire Chapters 5–6 argument is David's own transition. The verbs matter: heavens declare/shew/proclaim (announce) vs. law converts/makes wise/enlightens (transform).
  2. Psalm 29 — S3, cited by number. The Qôl Yahweh ("the voice of the LORD") psalm: seven thunderclaps of the divine voice over the waters, shaking the wilderness, breaking the cedars (Ps. 29:5), and the climax — "in his temple doth every one speak of his glory" (Ps. 29:9). Calvin reads the same pivot: storm-theophany → the Word spoken in the sanctuary.
  3. Psalm 93:5 — S4, quoted. "Thy testimonies are very sure; holiness becometh thine house for ever." The enthronement psalm whose first half is the raging sea (vv.3–4, "the floods lift up their waves") and whose conclusion turns to God's testimonies — the third witness to the same nature→Word movement.
  4. John 4:22 — S5, quoted (Christ to the Samaritan woman). "Ye worship ye know not what: we know what we worship: for salvation is of the Jews." The dominical seal on the thesis: worship without the Word = worship of an unknown object. Ties directly back to Acts 17:23 (the altar to the unknown God) and Calvin's I.5.12–13.
  5. Romans 1:20–21 — hovering behind S2 (the invitation ... proving of no avail) and S6 (labouring under vanity and error). ἐματαιώθησαν ("they became vain") is the Greek of vanity; the inexcusable of Rom. 1:20 is the flip side of the failed invitation — creation's witness leaves the nations without excuse precisely because it invites and is refused.
  6. Ecclesiastes 1:2 / Hebrew hebel — behind vanity in S6. Hebel = breath, vapour, emptiness. The heathen quest is not wicked exertion but empty exertion — toil that grasps at vapour.
  7. Psalm 119 — the great Torah-psalm standing behind Calvin's whole use of Psalm 19's second half; its relentless praise of statutes, testimonies, commandments, law is the expanded version of S1's four synonyms for the Word.
  8. Deuteronomy 4:6–8 — behind S5–S6, the Jews alone. "What nation is there so great, that hath statutes and judgments so righteous as all this law?" Israel's uniqueness in Deuteronomy is explicitly that it possesses the Word — exactly Calvin's ground for the exception.
  9. Isaiah 55:1–3 / John 6:44–45 — behind the premise of S6, unable to come to God if not aided. The prophetic-dominical theme that no one comes unless drawn and taught; Calvin's aided and upheld by his sacred word is the epistemic form of that dependence.

How Calvin uses Scripture here: count the moves. He does not argue; he stacks witnesses and lets their agreement do the work. Three psalms with an identical structure, then Christ himself — the repetition is a rhetorical proof: this is not one poet's quirk but the settled logic of revelation. And note his restraint: he quotes Psalm 19 in full but Psalms 29 and 93 by paraphrase-plus-tagline, trusting you to hear the pattern. When Calvin gives you three texts with the same shape, he is teaching you to read for structure, not just verses. That is exegesis as pedagogy.

8 — Exam Problems (the examiner's eye) –

① 어법 — Four underlined. One is wrong. Find it.

The prophet, ⓐafter mentioning that the heavens declare the glory of God and that the firmament ⓑsheweth forth his works, ⓒproceeding to make mention of the Word; and, the human mind ⓓbeing unable to come to God if not upheld by his word, it necessarily followed that the nations laboured under error.

Answer: ⓒ → must be proceeds (a finite main verb).

출제 의도: This is Formula 1, and the item tests whether you can locate the real main verb. The subject The prophet opens the sentence and then waits through the whole after-preamble (after mentioning that ... and that ...). Its predicate, when it finally arrives, must be finite: proceeds. Turn it into a participle (proceeding) and the prophet-clause has no main verb at all — the sentence becomes a heap of subordinate material with no spine. ⓐ after mentioning is correct (the gerund preamble). ⓑ sheweth is correct (archaic 3sg inside the that-clause, and it has a subject, the firmament). ⓓ being is correct — that is a legitimate nominative absolute (the human mind being unable...), which is supposed to be non-finite because the finite verb comes later (it necessarily followed).

The diagnostic: after an X, after V-ing ... opening, ask "where is the finite verb whose subject is X?" If the only candidate is a participle, the sentence is broken.

② 내용일치 — Which of the following agrees with the passage?

  1. Calvin cites Psalm 19 chiefly to prove that the heavens and firmament reveal God's glory to all nations.
  2. Calvin argues that the Jews reached the true God because their natural wisdom and devotion were superior to those of other nations.
  3. Calvin concedes that the law has uses beyond instruction, but for his present point treats it as the school in which God's children are trained, since the invitation of nature comes to nothing.
  4. Calvin holds that the words of Christ to the Samaritan woman show that all nations, including the Jews, worshipped an unknown God.

Answer: 3. (S2 — the conceded other uses besides, the narrowed general meaning = school, and the trailing absolute proving of no avail.)

출제 의도 (learn the diseases, not the answers):

  • 1 = the pivot trap, the single most attractive wrong answer. Calvin quotes Psalm 19 precisely to show that David turns from the heavens to the Word (proceeds to make mention of the Word). The point is the pivot, not the panorama. A reader who stops at the first half of S1 chooses 1 and feels safe. The verb proceeds is the whole answer — the citation is about the transition, general → special.
  • 2 = direction flip on the exception. S6 makes the Jews' exemption bibliological, not moral: they alone were aided ... by his sacred word. The premise says the human mind — any human mind — is altogether unable unaided. Any choice crediting Jewish wisdom or devotion inverts Calvin's actual claim, which is that no mind reaches God on its own.
  • 4 = scope violation on the Jews excepted. S5–S6 say all nations except the Jews worshipped they-knew-not-what. Choice 4 sweeps the Jews into the condemned group ("including the Jews"), erasing the one exception the whole verdict turns on. Whenever the text says "all X, the Y excepted," a choice that folds Y back into X is always wrong.

③ 영작 — Translate, using Formula 3 (if not = unless + it necessarily followed that) and a trailing absolute (Formula 2).

"인간의 마음은 그의 말씀으로 붙들어 주지 않으면 하나님께 이를 수 없으므로, 열방은 — 성경 없이 그를 찾았기에 — 헛됨 가운데서 수고하고 있었다는 결론이 필연적으로 따라 나왔다." (Since the human mind cannot reach God if not upheld by his word, it necessarily followed that the nations — seeking him without Scripture — were labouring under vanity.)

Model answer:

Since the human mind was unable to come to God if not upheld by his word, it necessarily followed that the nations, seeking him without the Word, were labouring under vanity and error.

출제 의도 & 채점 기준 (3 points):

  1. if not = unless, correctly placed: unable to come to God **if not upheld** by his word ✔ — a conditional of necessity. Writing "if he was not upheld" is acceptable; writing "except upheld" or dropping the negative (if upheld) reverses the meaning (0 points). The Word must be indispensable, not optional.
  2. The inference marker is exact: **it necessarily followed that** ... ✔ — the logical ergo. Do not soften it to "it probably followed" or "so the nations were..."; the register is entailment. Keep necessarily.
  3. The trailing absolute is well-formed: the nations, **seeking** him without the Word, were labouring... ✔ — a participle (seeking) supplying the reason, hung on the subject. And use labouring under (= burdened by), not "working on" — the whole point is that their toil was empty. Vanity = futility, not conceit.

(Also acceptable: ..., the nations being left to seek him without the Word, laboured under vanity and error — a fuller absolute. Full marks. Not acceptable: any version where if not is read as except, or where labouring under is rendered as diligent effort.)

9 — Wrap-up + Homework –

One-line summary: What Calvin argued in §§1–3, David and Christ argued first: the same prophet who sang the heavens declare the glory of God proceeds to the law of the Lord is perfect (Ps. 19), and Psalms 29 and 93 make the identical pivot — storm and sea giving way to temple and testimonies — while our Saviour seals it (John 4:22, the Jews alone); so, since the mind if not aided ... was altogether unable to come to God, it necessarily followed that all mankind, the Jews excepted, were labouring under vanity and error — Chapter 6's thesis proved from Scripture's own mouth.

The 3 formulas, one more time:

  1. SUBJECT, after V-ing [that-cl × 3], + MAIN VERB — the suspended preamble; the real main verb is proceeds, not the verbs trapped in the after-clause (S1).
  2. [main clause]; NOUN + present-participle — the trailing nominative absolute as verdict, fired three times: proving of no avail (S2), unbelievers being deaf (S3), the Jews excepted (S6).
  3. Since S was unable to V if not aided ..., it necessarily followed that ... — if not = unless (conditional of necessity); it necessarily followed = logical ergo; labour under = be burdened by (S6).

Homework (10 minutes):

  1. Structure restoration. Rebuild the four witnesses from memory (Ps 19, Ps 29, Ps 93, John 4) and, for each, name the two halves: what comes first (creation's glory) and what it pivots to (the Word). Then answer in one sentence: why does Calvin stack four witnesses instead of arguing? (Hint: what does agreement across four independent texts prove that a syllogism cannot?)
  2. Composition. Using Formula 1, write one sentence in which a subject waits through an after-preamble of three that-clauses before its main verb lands. Then underline your main verb and confirm its subject is the opening noun — not anything inside the after-clause.
  3. Reading. Read Psalm 19 aloud, all fourteen verses. Mark the exact seam where the heavens (vv.1–6) give way to the law (vv.7–9). That seam is Calvin's whole chapter. Then ask his question: by what does the law do what the heavens cannot — convert, make wise, enlighten? Your answer is why the school of the Word is necessary.
  4. Tomorrow's preview. §4 ends Chapter 6 with the verdict that everything hangs on Scripture. Which forces the next question, and Calvin knows it: Chapter 7 — "THE TESTIMONY OF THE SPIRIT NECESSARY TO GIVE FULL AUTHORITY TO SCRIPTURE." Its first section opens: "Before proceeding farther, it seems proper to make some observations on the authority of Scripture..." If the Word is the only road to God, how do we know the Word is God's? Come having asked yourself: what makes me certain the Bible is from heaven? Calvin's answer will not be the Church. It will be the Spirit.
